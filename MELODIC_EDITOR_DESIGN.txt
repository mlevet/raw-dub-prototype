RAW DUB PROTOTYPE - MELODIC EDITOR DESIGN
A deliberate design pause before implementing further pitch editing
=======================================================================

CONTEXT
-----------------------------------------------------------------------
The prototype currently has a uniform Step model (hit, level, pitch),
Ghost/Normal/Accent levels, per-step pitch on Bass, and a consistent
click-to-activate / drag-to-edit interaction language.

Before extending pitch editing further, we paused to ask a more
fundamental question: this instrument will not only contain drums
(Kick, Snare, Hat). It will centrally contain melodic and harmonic
instruments - Bass, Skank, Bubble, Chords, Lead, FX synth. Those are
not secondary. So the real question is not "how do we edit pitch" but:

    How do we compose melodies and harmony in a compact, immediate
    way that stays true to the instrument's philosophy?

The goal is a MELODIC EDITOR (not a "Bass editor") that eventually
serves Bass, Skank, Bubble, Lead, and FX synth with one shared
philosophy - the same way the step grid already serves Kick/Snare/Hat
with one shared philosophy.

WHY THE KORG SQ-1 IS READABLE
-----------------------------------------------------------------------
Studied not to copy, but to extract the underlying principle. The
SQ-1's readability comes from three things happening at once:

1. Pitch is a literal spatial/analog quantity (knob position), not a
   digit or note name.
2. The whole phrase is visible at once - no scrolling, no paging.
3. Because of (1) and (2), shape recognition happens BEFORE conscious
   reading - you see a melody rise and fall the way you see a
   mountain range, not the way you read a sentence.

Any melodic editor design that breaks one of these three properties
loses the thing that makes it work.

TWO ADDITIONAL DESIGN PRINCIPLES (confirmed after review)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------
  - The musician should be able to recognise a phrase from across the
    room. Meant literally: zoomed out, a melody should have a shape,
    the way you can recognise a waveform without reading it.
  - The editor should be learnable without a manual. Put another
    musician in front of it for ten minutes; they should discover how
    to write a bassline on their own. If it needs an explanation,
    it's too clever. This is the same standard that makes early
    Fruity Loops and the SQ-1 approachable despite not being
    feature-poor - the interaction matches the musician's mental
    model well enough that no manual is needed.

THREE INTERACTION MODELS CONSIDERED
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

1. SCALE-LOCKED BAR GRID (refinement of what's already built)
   -------------------------------------------------------------------
   Same bar-per-step, drag-to-set-height language as today, but
   height snaps to SCALE DEGREES within the song's Key, not raw
   chromatic semitones. Fill weight still carries Ghost/Normal/Accent
   exactly as already designed.

   Why scale-locking matters: chromatic pitch gives 24+ possible bar
   heights that all look similar, so the silhouette reads as noise.
   Diatonic pitch gives ~7 heights per octave, so two bars either
   match or don't - the eye can actually compare them. This is SQ-1
   properties #1 and #3.

   Framing matters here: this is not "you can't play wrong notes" -
   that reads as a toy. It's "the instrument encourages coherent
   melodies" - a genre-appropriate constraint, not a limitation
   apologised for. Raw Dub should feel opinionated, not childproofed.
   One track, one key, very little modulation is the musical reality
   of dub, not an arbitrary software restriction - a global Key is
   close to a property of the genre.

   Harmony extension: the chord ROOT uses this exact same bar -
   literally the same gesture, same visual language as Bass. Chord
   QUALITY (major/minor/7th/sus) defaults automatically from the
   scale degree via ordinary diatonic harmony (i minor, iv minor, V
   major, etc., depending on the Key). Most of the time you only ever
   set the root, and quality is correct by construction - "expose
   musical concepts, not MIDI events" taken seriously.

   The override must NOT be buried as an "advanced option" - dub
   producers do deliberately reach for minor/major/sus/7 for their
   colour, not by accident. One click on an already-placed root should
   cycle its quality (e.g. Fm -> F7 -> Fsus -> Fmaj -> Fm...). Default
   is automatic and correct; override is one click away, not hidden.

   Cost: requires a new small global concept - a single song KEY
   (e.g. "F minor"). Treated as a feature, not overhead: one global
   constant that simultaneously makes melody readable AND harmony
   musical is a strong signal it's the right abstraction.

   Evaluation against philosophy:
     immediate               - yes, near-zero change from current gesture
     compact                 - yes, same footprint as today's step row
     no menu diving           - yes
     visually readable        - strong (clean bar-chart silhouette)
     encourages musical thinking - strong (coherent melodies by
                                  construction, quality inferred from
                                  real harmony, one-click to override)
     not a traditional DAW    - yes
     years of evolution       - strong (same bar language extends to
                                  Lead/Bubble/FX; Harmony reuses it for root)

2. DISCRETE PITCH-LANE GRID ("false piano roll," scale degrees as rows)
   -------------------------------------------------------------------
   Instead of one bar whose height varies, render a fixed small grid
   - one row per scale degree across ~1.5 octaves - and a step lights
   up the exact row it occupies. The melody is literally connect-the-
   dots across steps, which is even more direct than inferring it
   from bar height.

   Evaluation: probably the single most legible option in isolation -
   closer to actually SEEING the pitch than inferring it. But it
   fails compactness and long-term evolution: once Bass, Skank,
   Bubble, and Lead all want their own lane visible at once, that's
   four 10-row grids stacked - a piano roll wearing a costume. Also
   drifts furthest toward "traditional DAW" of the three options,
   even though the underlying data is just as scale-locked as option 1.

3. FREEHAND CONTOUR DRAW (pencil the whole phrase's shape in one gesture)
   -------------------------------------------------------------------
   Drag once across all 16 steps like painting an automation curve;
   each step snaps to the nearest scale degree as the pointer crosses
   it. Potentially the fastest way to sketch "rises then falls."

   Evaluation: compact and readable exactly like option 1 (same bar
   rendering), but the INTERACTION itself is automation-lane behavior
   borrowed from a DAW. Risks encouraging gestural doodling over the
   deliberate, one-decision-per-step thinking the rest of the
   instrument is built around - closer to the instrument composing
   for you than you composing on it. Does not unify with Harmony at
   all (can't freehand-draw a sequence of chord qualities), so it
   would live outside the "one family, one philosophy" goal rather
   than inside it. Worth keeping in mind as a possible LATER
   power-gesture layered on top of option 1, not as a foundation.

RECOMMENDATION
-----------------------------------------------------------------------
Option 1 - Scale-locked bar grid.

Reasons:
  - Smallest change from what already exists (scale-quantize the bar
    height already built for Bass).
  - The only option with a real, non-bolted-on answer for Harmony.
  - The only option that stays compact once four melodic voices need
    to coexist on screen at once.
  - Its one real cost - a global song Key - is arguably wanted
    independently of this decision, since a dub track lives in one
    key for its entire duration; this isn't a UI convenience, it's
    true of the genre.

OPEN SUB-PROBLEMS (deliberately left unsolved for now)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Octave range: how a bar is represented when it wants to go beyond
    the visible lane height. Explicitly NOT to be designed speculatively
    - do not invent octave switching yet. Wait until real basslines
    have actually been written (ten or so) and let the need reveal
    what feels natural, rather than guessing ahead of use.
  - How and where the Key gets set - this itself needs "musical
    language, not software language" treatment, not a dropdown of
    note names and mode names.
  - The exact gesture for the chord-quality override. Decided: it
    must be one click on the root, not buried as an advanced option
    (see Chord quality section above). Not yet decided: the precise
    interaction mechanics of that click/cycle.

These are intentionally not solved in this document. The point of
this design pause was to settle the MODEL, not the pixel-level
interaction - that's the next design session, once this direction is
confirmed.
